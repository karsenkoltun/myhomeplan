## Summer in the Okanagan is Beautiful - and Hard on Your Home

Okanagan summers are what make people move here. The lakes, the patios, the vineyards, the endless sunshine - there is a reason the valley's population keeps growing. But that same spectacular summer is also putting your home through one of its toughest seasons.

Between June and August, Kelowna, West Kelowna, Penticton, Vernon, Lake Country, and the surrounding communities experience average highs above 30 degrees Celsius, extended periods above 35, and occasional days pushing past 40. Combine that heat with near-zero rainfall, active wildfire seasons, intense UV exposure, and the heaviest outdoor use your property sees all year, and you have a season that demands serious attention.

This is not a generic summer checklist. Every item below is calibrated to the Okanagan Valley's specific conditions - our heat, our drought, our fire risk, and our lifestyle. Follow it from June through August, and your home will come out of summer in better shape than it went in.

## Air Conditioning: Your Summer Lifeline

### Pre-Season AC Service

If you only do one thing from this entire checklist, make it this: get your air conditioning serviced before summer hits. In the Okanagan, your AC is not a luxury - it is a survival system. When temperatures hit 38 degrees for days on end, a failed AC unit is not just uncomfortable, it is a health risk, particularly for children, elderly family members, and pets.

Schedule a professional AC service in May or early June. A proper service includes checking refrigerant levels and pressures, cleaning the evaporator and condenser coils, inspecting electrical connections and capacitors, testing the thermostat, lubricating moving parts, and verifying proper airflow.

The cost of a pre-season AC tune-up runs $100 to $200. The cost of an emergency AC repair on a 38-degree day in July - when every HVAC technician in the Okanagan is booked solid - can easily run $500 to $1,500 or more.

### Ongoing AC Maintenance Through Summer

**Change your filter monthly during heavy use.** When your AC runs 12 to 16 hours a day through July and August, the filter gets dirty fast. A clogged filter forces the system to work harder, increases energy consumption by 5 to 15 percent, and reduces cooling capacity right when you need it most.

**Keep the outdoor condenser unit clear.** Your condenser needs unobstructed airflow to function efficiently. Clear any debris, grass clippings, or vegetation within two feet of the unit. Gently rinse the condenser fins with a garden hose (never a pressure washer) every few weeks to remove dust and pollen.

**Set your thermostat strategically.** Every degree below 24 increases your cooling costs by approximately 3 to 5 percent. Set your thermostat to 24 to 25 degrees when you are home and 27 to 28 degrees when you are away. A programmable or smart thermostat automates this and can reduce your summer cooling bill by 10 to 20 percent.

**Use ceiling fans to supplement.** Ceiling fans make a room feel 3 to 4 degrees cooler through wind-chill effect. This allows you to set your thermostat higher while maintaining comfort. Make sure fans rotate counterclockwise in summer to push air downward.

<Callout type="tip">
Close blinds and curtains on south and west facing windows during the afternoon. Solar heat gain through windows accounts for up to 30 percent of your cooling load. Reflective window film is a permanent solution that pays for itself within two summers in the Okanagan.
</Callout>

## Deck and Patio Maintenance

Your deck and patio are the centre of Okanagan summer living. From the first warm evening in May to the last barbecue in September, these outdoor spaces get more use than any room in your house. They also take more punishment.

### Inspection and Repair

**Inspect structural elements first.** Before loading your deck with furniture, a hot tub, and a crowd of guests, check the structural integrity. Look underneath for signs of rot in posts, beams, and joists. Wiggle railings to check for looseness. Look for popped or corroded fasteners. Any structural concerns should be addressed by a professional before the deck is used for entertaining.

**Check for surface damage.** Walk the entire deck surface looking for cracked, warped, or splintered boards. Sand down splinters and replace any boards that are compromised. A single rotten board left unaddressed will spread decay to adjacent boards.

**Tighten all hardware.** Screws and bolts loosen over time due to wood expansion and contraction. Go through every accessible fastener and tighten anything loose. Replace any fasteners that are corroded or stripped.

### Cleaning and Protection

**Power wash your deck.** A thorough pressure washing in late May or early June removes winter grime, mildew, and the grey oxidation layer that forms on exposed wood. Use appropriate pressure settings - too much pressure can damage wood fibres and create a fuzzy surface. Cedar and softwoods need lower pressure than hardwoods or composite materials.

**Stain or seal your deck.** After pressure washing and allowing 48 to 72 hours of drying time, apply a quality exterior stain or sealant. The Okanagan's intense UV exposure breaks down surface treatments faster than coastal or shaded environments. A semi-transparent stain with UV blockers offers the best protection while still showing the wood grain.

In the Okanagan, you should expect to restain your deck every 2 to 3 years. South-facing decks and surfaces that receive all-day sun may need attention every 1 to 2 years.

**Protect furniture contact points.** Place protective pads under the legs of heavy furniture, planters, and grills. Standing water under planters causes localized rot that can destroy a deck board in a single season.

<Callout type="warning">
Never place a barbecue or fire pit directly on a wood deck without a protective mat or pad rated for high temperatures. The Okanagan's dry summer conditions make deck fires a real risk. Keep a fire extinguisher accessible whenever you are grilling.
</Callout>

### Composite and Vinyl Decking

If you have composite or vinyl decking - increasingly common in new Okanagan construction - maintenance is simpler but not zero. Clean the surface with a composite deck cleaner and soft brush. Do not use a pressure washer above 1,500 PSI. Check expansion gaps between boards and clear any debris that has accumulated in the gaps, as blockages can cause buckling in extreme heat.

## Irrigation and Water Management

Water is the most precious resource in the Okanagan summer, and managing it properly is both an environmental responsibility and a financial one. Municipal water rates in Kelowna, Penticton, Vernon, and West Kelowna increase during summer months, and water restrictions are common during drought years.

### Irrigation System Check

**Run every zone and inspect.** Walk your property while each irrigation zone runs. Look for broken sprinkler heads, misaligned spray patterns, areas of overspray hitting hardscaping, and dry spots that indicate blocked or damaged heads.

**Check for leaks.** A small leak in an underground irrigation line can waste thousands of litres over a summer while going completely unnoticed. Read your water meter before and after a period when no water is being used. If the meter moves, you have a leak somewhere in the system.

**Adjust head positions.** Over winter, frost heave can shift sprinkler heads out of alignment. Reposition any heads that are spraying onto driveways, sidewalks, or fences instead of your lawn and garden beds.

### Smart Watering Practices

**Water early.** Set your irrigation to run between 5 and 8 AM. Watering during the heat of the day loses 30 to 50 percent of your water to evaporation. Evening watering leaves moisture on grass blades overnight, promoting fungal disease.

**Water deeply, not frequently.** Deep watering 2 to 3 times per week encourages roots to grow deeper into the soil, making your lawn more drought-resistant. Daily light watering creates shallow root systems that wilt at the first sign of heat stress.

**Follow water restrictions.** Most Okanagan municipalities implement staged water restrictions during summer. Know your watering days and times. Fines for violating restrictions range from $100 to $500 depending on your municipality and the restriction level.

## Lawn Care Through the Okanagan Heat

Maintaining a healthy lawn through an Okanagan summer requires adjusting nearly every aspect of your lawn care routine compared to spring and fall.

### Mowing

**Raise your mowing height to 3 to 3.5 inches.** This is the single most important summer lawn care adjustment. Taller grass shades the soil surface, reduces water evaporation by up to 30 percent, and keeps root temperatures lower. Short-mowed lawns in the Okanagan summer are a recipe for brown, stressed turf.

**Mow less frequently.** When temperatures consistently exceed 30 degrees, cool-season grasses slow their growth dramatically. You may only need to mow every 10 to 14 days during the hottest periods, compared to every 5 to 7 days in spring.

**Mow in the morning or evening.** Never mow during the midday heat. The combination of cutting stress and extreme heat can damage or kill sections of your lawn. Early morning after the dew has dried or early evening are ideal.

**Leave clippings on the lawn.** Grass clippings return nitrogen and moisture to the soil as they decompose. In the dry Okanagan summer, this free mulch and fertilizer is too valuable to bag and throw away.

### Dealing with Heat Stress and Dormancy

If your lawn turns brown during an extended heat wave, do not panic. Cool-season grasses common in the Okanagan are designed to go dormant during extreme heat as a survival mechanism. The grass is not dead - it is conserving energy and water by shutting down above-ground growth.

When your lawn goes dormant, stop mowing and reduce watering to once per week at most. The goal is to keep the crown of the grass plant alive, not to maintain a green appearance. Your lawn will recover and green up when temperatures moderate in September.

The worst thing you can do is alternate between heavy watering and no watering during dormancy. This forces the grass to break dormancy and then re-enter it, which exhausts the plant's energy reserves and can actually kill it.

## Pest Prevention and Management

Summer in the Okanagan brings a predictable lineup of household pests. Proactive prevention is far more effective and cheaper than reactive treatment.

### Common Summer Pests in the Okanagan

**Ants.** Carpenter ants and pavement ants are extremely common throughout Kelowna, Vernon, and Penticton. Carpenter ants are the bigger concern because they excavate wood for nesting, potentially causing structural damage. Look for sawdust-like frass near wooden structures, especially around decks, fence posts, and the base of your home.

**Wasps and hornets.** Yellow jackets and paper wasps build nests in eaves, soffits, wall cavities, and underground burrows. Early summer is when queens establish new colonies - a single nest removed in June prevents thousands of wasps by August.

**Spiders.** The Okanagan has an active spider population, including occasional sightings of hobo spiders and black widows. Keep exterior lights off or switch to yellow bulbs that attract fewer insects, which in turn attracts fewer spiders.

**Mosquitoes.** Standing water anywhere on your property breeds mosquitoes. Dump birdbaths weekly, clean gutters, fix leaky outdoor faucets, and ensure drainage is functioning properly. The Okanagan's dry climate actually reduces mosquito populations compared to wetter regions, but any standing water negates that advantage.

### Prevention Strategies

**Seal entry points.** Inspect the exterior of your home for gaps around pipes, vents, electrical conduit, windows, and doors. Seal any gap larger than 3 millimetres with caulking or expandable foam. Pay special attention to where the foundation meets the siding.

**Eliminate food sources.** Keep outdoor garbage cans sealed with tight-fitting lids. Clean your barbecue after every use. Pick up fallen fruit from fruit trees - the Okanagan's famous cherry, peach, and apple trees are a pest magnet when fruit drops and ferments on the ground.

**Trim vegetation away from your home.** Maintain a 12-inch gap between your home's exterior and any plants or shrubs. Branches touching your home provide a highway for ants, spiders, and other pests to enter.

## Fire Safety and Wildfire Preparation

This section deserves its own detailed post, but no summer maintenance checklist for the Okanagan is complete without covering the basics of wildfire preparation. The 2023 and 2024 fire seasons reminded every Okanagan homeowner that this is not a theoretical risk.

### Maintain Your Defensible Space

The FireSmart program recommends three priority zones around your home:

**Zone 1 (0-1.5 metres from the home):** This is the most critical area. It should be a non-combustible zone. Use gravel, stone, or concrete rather than bark mulch. Remove any combustible items including firewood stacks, patio furniture cushions, and recycling bins. Clean all organic debris from the roof, gutters, and beneath decks.

**Zone 2 (1.5-10 metres from the home):** Space trees so their crowns are at least 3 metres apart. Remove lower branches to a height of 2 metres. Keep grass mowed to 10 centimetres or less. Store firewood at the far edge of this zone, uphill from the house if possible.

**Zone 3 (10-30 metres from the home):** Thin trees and remove dead or dying vegetation. Create breaks in continuous vegetation. The goal is to slow an approaching fire and reduce its intensity.

### Summer Fire Safety Practices

**Clean your gutters of dry needles and leaves.** Gutters full of dry pine needles are essentially a fuse that can carry fire directly to your roof. If you cleaned your gutters in spring, check them again in July - Ponderosa pine needles fall year-round in the Okanagan.

**Keep outdoor areas clear of combustibles.** During peak fire season, store cushions, umbrellas, and other combustible patio items inside or in a fire-resistant storage area when not in use. A single ember landing on a cushion stored against your house can ignite your siding.

**Have an evacuation plan.** Know your routes, have a go-bag packed, keep important documents in a fireproof safe or digitized in cloud storage, and ensure every family member knows the plan. When an evacuation order comes in the Okanagan, you may have minutes, not hours.

<Callout type="warning">
During extreme fire weather, keep windows and doors closed to prevent ember entry, ensure garden hoses are connected and accessible, move propane tanks away from structures, and park vehicles facing outward for quick departure. Monitor the BC Wildfire Service and your regional district emergency alerts continuously.
</Callout>

## Outdoor Entertaining Preparation

Summer in the Okanagan means hosting. Whether it is a deck dinner party, a poolside barbecue, or a backyard gathering, your outdoor entertaining spaces need maintenance to stay functional, safe, and attractive all season.

### Outdoor Kitchen and Barbecue

**Deep clean your barbecue at the start of summer.** Remove grates and heat plates, scrub the firebox, clean the burner ports with a thin wire, check gas connections with soapy water for leaks, and inspect the ignition system. A neglected barbecue is both a health and fire hazard.

**Clean your barbecue after every use.** This is not about aesthetics - food residue attracts pests and becomes increasingly difficult to remove as it carbonizes.

### Outdoor Lighting

**Test and maintain all exterior lighting.** Replace burned-out bulbs, clean light fixtures of bugs and debris, and ensure pathway lights and deck lights are functional. Good lighting extends your entertaining hours and prevents trips and falls.

**Consider solar pathway lights.** The Okanagan gets more than enough sunshine to power solar lights reliably all summer. They add ambiance without increasing your electricity bill.

### Pool and Hot Tub

If you have a pool or hot tub, summer maintenance is a weekly commitment. Test water chemistry at least twice per week during heavy use. Clean filters regularly, skim debris daily, and ensure the pump and heater are functioning properly. The Okanagan's cottonwood fluff in June and July is particularly challenging for pool owners - skim baskets may need daily emptying.

## Exterior Surface Protection

The Okanagan sun is relentless. UV exposure fades paint, cracks sealants, warps wood, and degrades roofing materials faster than most other climates in Canada.

### Paint and Siding

**Inspect painted surfaces for peeling, blistering, or fading.** The south and west sides of your home take the most UV punishment. Touch up problem areas immediately - exposed wood absorbs moisture and UV, accelerating deterioration.

**Check vinyl siding for warping.** Extreme heat can cause vinyl siding to warp, especially on south-facing walls. If you notice warping, it may indicate insufficient insulation or ventilation behind the siding.

### Windows

**Professional window cleaning removes mineral deposits and hard water stains** that build up from irrigation overspray and dried rain. These deposits etch into glass over time and become permanent if left untreated. Schedule a cleaning in early summer and again in early fall.

**Check window screens.** Inspect all window screens for tears, holes, or damaged frames. Screens are your first line of defense against summer insects, and even a small tear renders them ineffective.
